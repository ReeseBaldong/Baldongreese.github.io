Irena Sendler

The rescuer of the Warsow Ghetto children

Here's a time line of Ms. Sendler's life:

  • February 15, 1910 - Born in Warsaw, Poland
  • 1917 - Her father, a doctor, died during the typhus epidemic, leaving a lasting impression on Irena about the importance of helping others.
  • 1932 - Pursued a degree in Polish literature and then later switched to social work, earning her degree from the university of Warsaw. Began working as a social worker, focusing on aiding families and children in need, regardless of religion or background.
  • 1939 - Germany invaded poland and still continued her social work, joining the Polish underground resistance movement.
  • 1940 - Sendler, along with a network of colleagues, began organizing efforts to rescue Jewish children from the ghetto. Used her position as social worker to gain access to the ghetto under the guise of conducting inspection to prevent the spread of disease.
  • 1942 - Became a key member of the Zegota organization, a Polish underground resistance group focused on aiding Jews. She led the children's section of Zegota, which specialized in rescuing Jewish children.
  • 1942 - Smuggled children out of the ghetto in various ways, including through sewers, secret passage, and hiding them in bags and boxes. Once outside the ghetto, the children were placed with Polish families, orphanages, or convents, assuming new identities to hide their Jewish heritage.
  • 1942 - Despite the risk, she continued her efforts and was estimated that her network rescued around 2,500 Jewish children, risking their own lives to do so. Sendler kept records of the children's identities, hoping to one day reunite them with their families.
  • 1943 - Sendler was arrested by the Gestapo and brutally interrogated. Despite facing torture, she refused to betray her colleagues or the children she rescued. she was sentenced to death but managed to escape execution due to bribes paid by Zegota members.
  • 1945 - After the war, Sendler worked tirelessly to reunite Jewish children with their families. Tragically, many of the children's parents had been killed in the holocaust. Sendler continued her social work, aiding survivors and adavocating for human rights.
  • Early 2000s - her heroic actions and her role in rescuing Jewish children were bought to light when a group of students from Uniontown, Kansas, discovered her story while researching for a project on the Holocaust.
  • 2007 - She was nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize. Although she did not win, her legacy lives on as a symbol of courage, compassion, and resistance against justice.
  • May 12, 2008 - Irena Sendler (Single by choice) passed away at the age of 98. She received numerous posthumous honors for her wartime efforts, including the Order of the White Eagle, Poland's highest civilian decoration.

"Every child saved with my help is the justification of my existence on this earth, and not a title to glory."

-- Irena Sendler
